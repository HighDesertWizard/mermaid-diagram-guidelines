# James Flynn and the Cognitive Science Behind MDG

*James Robert Flynn (1934–2020)*
*University of Otago, Dunedin, New Zealand*

---

## The Discovery

In the early 1980s, a political philosopher at a university in New Zealand noticed something that the entire field of intelligence research had missed.

James Flynn was studying IQ data from military conscription records across dozens of countries when he found a pattern so consistent it seemed impossible: every generation scored significantly higher than the one before. Not by a little. By a lot — roughly 3 points per decade, sustained across the entire 20th century, in every industrialized nation with reliable data.

This wasn't supposed to happen. IQ tests are designed so that 100 is always the average. But when Flynn compared raw scores across generations — giving a 1980s test to people and comparing their performance to 1950s norms — the gains were enormous. A person scoring average in 1980 would have scored in the top 2% by 1900 standards.

The intelligence research community initially resisted the finding. Then they confirmed it. Then they named it after him: **the Flynn Effect**.

## What the Gains Were — and Weren't

The gains weren't uniform across all types of thinking. Flynn found that scores on vocabulary, arithmetic, and general knowledge — the kinds of intelligence that reflect what you've been taught — showed modest improvement at best.

The dramatic gains were concentrated in **abstract reasoning**. Tests like Raven's Progressive Matrices, which measure the ability to identify patterns in novel visual arrangements, showed the largest increases. People weren't learning more facts. They were getting better at a specific kind of thinking.

This led Flynn to his deepest question: what changed?

## The Three Pillars

Flynn identified three cognitive abilities that modern environments exercise in ways that pre-modern environments did not. He laid these out across his published work — particularly *What Is Intelligence?* (2007) and *Are We Getting Smarter?* (2012) — and in his 2013 TED talk, ["Why our IQ levels are higher than our grandparents'."](https://www.ted.com/talks/james_flynn_why_our_iq_levels_are_higher_than_our_grandparents)

### 1. Classification

The ability to organize the world into abstract categories rather than by concrete, practical association.

Flynn drew on Alexander Luria's research with pre-modern rural populations to illustrate this. When asked "What do a crow and a fish have in common?", people in pre-industrial communities would answer practically — "a crow eats a fish" or "you can catch both." They organized the world by what things *do* in relation to each other.

Modern thinkers default to taxonomic classification: "They're both animals." This seems obvious to us, but it represents a fundamentally different cognitive operation — placing things into abstract hierarchies rather than relating them through lived experience.

### 2. Using Abstractions

The ability to reason with abstract concepts and make them logically consistent, even when divorced from concrete experience.

Flynn illustrated this with syllogism tests. When presented with:

> *"There are no camels in Germany. Hamburg is in Germany. Are there camels in Hamburg?"*

Pre-modern subjects would often respond: "I've never been to Hamburg. I don't know." They weren't being obtuse — they were applying a perfectly rational epistemology: don't make claims about things you haven't directly observed.

Modern thinkers engage with the formal structure of the argument. We accept the premises and follow the logic, regardless of whether we have personal experience of Hamburg or its camel population. We treat the abstraction as real enough to reason about.

### 3. Taking the Hypothetical Seriously

The willingness to reason about things that aren't physically present — to engage with counterfactuals, theoretical scenarios, and systems that exist only as mental models.

This is the cognitive ability that underpins scientific reasoning, moral philosophy, strategic planning, and systems thinking. It's what lets us ask "What would happen if...?" and treat the question as meaningful rather than idle.

Flynn argued that modern education, complex workplaces, and technology-rich environments don't make us biologically smarter — they exercise these three cognitive muscles. The analogy he used was physical: just as better nutrition and training made people taller and stronger without changing the underlying genetics, cognitively demanding environments strengthened abstract thinking without changing the underlying neural architecture.

## Why This Matters for Diagrams

The connection between Flynn's three pillars and diagramming is not a metaphor. It's structural.

A well-made diagram is an act of **classification** — shapes and colors encode categories, turning implicit groupings into explicit visual taxonomy. Rectangles mean one thing, circles mean another, color families delineate domains.

A well-made diagram is an act of **abstraction** — hierarchical nesting and subgraph containers take relationships that exist only conceptually and make them spatially navigable. You can see containment, dependency, and flow without holding the entire system in working memory.

A well-made diagram is an act of **hypothetical reasoning** — it makes invisible structures visible. Dependencies, gaps, feedback loops, and consequences that aren't apparent from text alone become spatial objects you can point at and reason about. A diagram lets you think about a system that doesn't physically exist in front of you.

This is why a well-structured diagram isn't just a communication tool. It's a **cognitive amplifier** — it exercises the same three abilities that drove a century of rising human intelligence.

And this is why MDG exists. When an LLM generates a broken, flat, unstyled diagram, it's not just ugly — it's failing to function as the cognitive tool it could be. MDG ensures that LLM-generated diagrams actually operationalize Flynn's three pillars: clear classification through visual encoding, navigable abstraction through hierarchy, and visible structure through spatial layout.

## His Legacy

James Flynn was born in Washington, D.C. in 1934. He emigrated to New Zealand in 1963 and spent the rest of his career at the University of Otago in Dunedin, where he served as Foundation Professor of Political Studies and later Emeritus Professor. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society of New Zealand and received an honorary doctorate from the University of Otago in 2010.

He published prolifically across intelligence research, political philosophy, and civil liberties. His major works include *Race, IQ and Jensen* (1980), *What Is Intelligence? Beyond the Flynn Effect* (2007), *Are We Getting Smarter? Rising IQ in the Twenty-First Century* (2012), and *A Book Too Risky to Publish: Free Speech and Universities* (2019).

He died on December 11, 2020, in Dunedin, at the age of 86.

The Flynn Effect remains one of the most robust findings in the history of psychology — documented in over 30 countries, replicated across decades of data, and still generating active research into why some populations now show signs of reversal. Flynn himself was characteristically unsentimental about it: he was less interested in the fact that scores rose than in *what that rising told us about the relationship between minds and the worlds they inhabit*.
